Looking Back and Ahead With
Compass' Fourth-Quarter 2019 Market Reports
 
The most recent Compass market reports are in, and the results can be described as cautiously optimistic as the market, especially in Manhattan, is seeing some modest gains. It's certainly not a complete rebound but as the decade has come to an end it is worthwhile to keep some perspective of where we are compared to where we were.  Yes, we are in the midst of a slowdown from the most recent peak years, but we are way better off than when the decade began. 
 
The Manhattan Q4 2019 report describes the mood of late last year as "cautious stability," and it fits. Consider that the first part of the decade, just after the 2008 financial crisis, was marked by exceedingly slow sales volume. While many have called the recent slowdown in Manhattan transactions an indication of another economic downturn, it's important to note that even at the lowest point last year, prices were still 15 percent higher than 10 years ago and deals are happening. It just takes longer for buyers and sellers to get on the same page!
 
Here is the encouraging news from this past quarter, sales are up 1%, prices bounced back some, inventory is lower, and mortgage rates are steady. The Q4 2019 median sales price in Manhattan was $999,000 (up 3 percent from Q3) while the average price per square foot dipped 2.5 percent to $1,333. While the year started out very rocky it started to show some better signs as the year went on. 
 
According to the Brooklyn Q4 2019 report, "New developments and the general expansion of Brooklyn are driving changes in price." The median sales price in the borough ticked up 2.5 percent from last quarter to $800,000. With a number of contracts in the pipeline, expect the Brooklyn market to start strong this year. 
 
The Queens Q4 2019 report shows that the borough is chugging right along without Amazon, thankyouverymuch. Median sales price leapt 8.6 percent to $585,493 last quarter, and average price per square foot jumped 8.2 percent to $710.
 
While trade concerns and the traditional election-year uncertainty may toy with 2020 performance, the local economy is strong and interest rates are at record lows. Modest, but steady, growth seems in the cards for the city as a whole this year. Empirically to start the year we are seeing more activity at open houses and speaking to our mortgage friends they are seeing a robust amount of applications for pre qualification/approvals which is always a good sign... 
 
Ready? Set? Restaurant Week!
 
Few things make me as happy as Restaurant Week in New York City! Running now until Feb. 9, the winter edition of this biannual extravaganza offers two-course prix-fixe lunches for $26 and three-course dinners $42 for more than 365 establishments (tax, tip, and drinks not included). It's the perfect opportunity to try new spots on your must-try list and for revisiting old favorites. Confounded by the sheer number of options? No worries! The experts have chimed in with some solid recommendations.
 
Forbes provides a solid roundup of Restaurant Week newbies that warrant your attention, including Cecconi’s Dumbo where wonderful seafood selections come with a side of waterfront views. Little Italy's Gran Tivoli is a great option for vegan/vegetarian fare while the Flatiron or Battery Park City Blue Smoke outposts are a natural for carnivores. 
 
The Thrillist, meanwhile, encourages readers to use Restaurant Week as an opportunity to try restaurants you might not normally choose. Maybe that's ritzy stalwart 21 Club, the St. Regis Hotel’s luxe Astor Court or Time Warner Center's Bluebird London, featuring stunning Central Park vistas.
 
Bon appetit!
Changes, both big and small, are coming to New York City in 2020, and many are focused squarely on sustainability and conservation. 
 
Single-Use Plastic Bags: New Yorkers are known for our ability to schlep our purchases and possessions everywhere, but no longer will we do so in single-use plastic bags. As of March 2020, businesses will be forbidden from doling out these environmental disasters. And that's a good thing: New Yorkers use roughly 23 million plastic bags a year, and half of them end up in waterways and landfills. Tote bag chic is about to get real (consult this Racked list of the most iconic New York versions to get ahead of the game). 
 
MetroCards: MetroCards will soon go the way of the subway token thanks to RFID contactless "tap" technology. The MTA's new fare system, called OMNY, is rolling out readers throughout the city's buses and subways that allow riders to pay using credit/debit/prepaid cards and phones or watches equipped with Apple Pay, Samsung Pay or Google Pay. Subway implementation is expected to be completed by the end of 2020, and you can check the progress on your local line here. No more MetroCard litter, and even better, no more waiting in line to juice up your card!
 
Green Grades: Starting this year, buildings over 25,000 square feet will be required to post a rating of their overall energy efficiency, not unlike those restaurant Health Department grades we've become accustomed to. Will shame be the secret weapon in encouraging building owners to make energy-conscious upgrades? That remains to be seen. (Another environmentally friendly move requires buildings to use bird-friendly glass starting at the end of the year.)
 
Other major changes in the city this year include big moves for women in the athletic and legislative arenas; new attractions coming to Broadway, Coney Island and Goshen (hello, Legoland!); plus transit improvements at Penn Station and across the subway system. 2020 looks like a great year in the making!
